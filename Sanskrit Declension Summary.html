Sanskrit Declension Summary

संग्रहो विभक्तीनां संस्कृतस्य

The Sanskrit declension system is rather complex, and unfortunately most resources seem to either focus on the a-stems (indeed the largest class of nouns, but also the most different from the others) and neglect the other noun classes, or provide lengthy tables and details about all the declensions without pointing out the similarities – of which there are in fact quite a few, as the following table attempts to emphasize.

The declension paradigms are those of Classical Sanskrit, and are based on Sanskrit Grammar by William Dwight Whitney.

General Notes

  • Masculines and Feminines of the same stem type generally decline the same. Exceptions are the i- and u-stems, shown below separately, and the Accusative Plural of all vowel stems.
  • Neuters take the same case endings as Masculines except in the Nominative and Accusative. They are therefore omitted from the main table.
  • Effects of internal Sandhi are not marked below. These generally involve the changes n → ṇ and s → ṣ.
  • Forms which are identical throughout the declension are merged, e.g. the Ablative and Genitive Singular of all declensions other than the a-stems.
  • The visarga ḥ is used for final -s and -r, since this is the ending encountered in isolation.
  • Case-forms are given in IAST transcript, since this makes it possible to mark consonants and vowels separately (browsers don't deal well with it with Devanagari conjuncts).
  • Again due to rendering technicalities, accent is currently not marked in the case forms.

Color Markings

  • Green marks the regular endings – generally those of the consonant stems, appearing also in most other cases.
  • Red marks irregular endings.
  • Orange marks alternative endings taken by long-vowel stems (generally feminine) in some Singular cases.
  • Purple indicates a stem change before the ending – mostly a vowel Lengthened, Gunated or Vrddhied.
  • Blue marks an inserted element, such as n or y.
  • Strong and Middle cases, where relevant, are marked by the background color. The other cases in the same declensions are weak.

start-footnotes The pronominal declension is characterized by the inserted elements sm/sy and some irregular endings, and is followed by pronouns and a few adjectives. Most of those exhibit additional irregularities, which anya does not.
Before vowel endings, polysyllabic stems in ī and ū regularly drop the final vowel and leave a semi-vowel; monosyllabic stems just shorten the final vowel.
Specifically, -an stem, which in particular has separate stem forms for the strong, middle and weak cases.
  The consonantal declension has several sub-groups which differ in the formation of stems and of the Nominative Singular; details can be found in the Little Red Book, for instance.
The regular ending -i combines with the stem-final -a to form the actual ending -e.
Most ṛ-stems have their strong stem in -ār, the others in -ar; all form the Locative Singular in -ar.
The vocative is technically the bare stem, as can be seen in the consonant stems; however, all short vowel stems gunate their final vowel.
The masculine and feminine are identical in these cases, as a result of the regular vowel changes in the a-stems and ā-stems respectively.
Masculines end in -n while feminines end in -ḥ throughout all declensions (except the consonantal one where both genders take -aḥ). For instance, the feminine ṛ-stem mātṛ Mother has the accusative plural mātṝḥ.
The ending is properly -ām; but all vowel stems precede it with -n-, and short vowels are lengthened.
